Kind of, especially traditional LLMs, but the newest ones using real reinforcement learning are a little different. This approach is still very new (not RL itself, but getting it to actually work well in LLMs is), where DeepSeek's R1 was most people's first exposure, since OpenAI's o1 was paywalled (I think o3-mini has a small amount of usage available in the free tier now).

In that training approach, a question is given to an LLM for which there is a known answer (say a math problem or code which has unit tests). It is run many times and prompted to separate out the "reasoning" from the final answer shown to the user. The "chains of thought" that lead to a correct answer are encouraged in an automated way. This process is run over and over and the reasoning portion gets longer and longer as more training happens, giving more consistently good answers. And emergent in that process, it starts to do things like second guess itself and backtrack and re-check previous steps and try the problem from multiple angles. It goes from just simulating internet documents and human labelers, to simulating problem solving techniques.

As that goes on, it can start to drift from the training data to whatever happens to work to get the right answer. When AlphaGo was first just trained on human games, it got up to a high level but could never beat the best players. When they started doing RL, having it play against itself, that is what led to Move 37 and actually beating the top human players. So right now we're just at the start of seeing where that can lead with LLMs. Highly recommend this video that was released a few days ago. The best overview on the past and current state of LLMs that I've ever seen: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7xTGNNLPyMI

Shawn, when you say "kind of" in response to the critique that LLMs cannot reason, I think you are downplaying a clear and extremely important distinction. tl;dr spelling that out to follow.

I've spent nearly 30 years now thinking and writing about algorithms like the self play you referenced and have a few thoughts on the matter. In the realm of game playing Arthur Samuels pioneered learning by self play at IBM in the 1950s and 1960s using the game of checkers. I tracked the work on Go the folks at (what would become) DeepMind were doing from around 2005 onward, as it meshed quite well with my own work and the results they were getting were pretty interesting. I experimented on small toy models in my PhD, so seeing similar ideas working on big games was cool to me at the time. Back then I might have been excited about LLMs and RL-based "reasoning".

I am not, because one of the conclusions I've come to is that we need to be cautious when comparing so-called reasoning algorithms to human reasoning. Such algorithms apply in what you might call small worlds, colloquially called games, where the possible configurations of the world can be fully described in advance, and where the possible actions one can take in any given configuration are fixed and known (or at least knowable). By contrast, human reasoning occurs in what one might call a large world, where *neither* of those assumptions hold. It's hard to overstate how big an error it is to confuse one for the other: it's a failure at the philosophical level, the technical level, even the emotional level.

To me that's one of the most heartbreaking things about the enthusiasm for generative AI right now: it's cementing this category error into the average person's thinking by making it easy for them to conflate two dramatically different situations that I think they'd be better off keeping separate in their minds. Take note that this isn't about the algorithm, whether it's RL or something else, but the framing: no algorithm, no matter how fast or thorough, no matter how much data it has or how great its theoretical grounding is, can overcome the small/large world gap. This is a space in which Searle's Chinese Room argument applies and Mary's room applies. This is related to what the philosopher Graham Harman has called undermining and overmining in his critiques of literalist worldviews (for instance). I'd strongly urge anyone who wants to seriously argue that LLMs can do something approximating reasoning to read and think about these authors' works. I don't think they've really grappled with what such a claim means otherwise.

I don't know about recent versions, but based on Wolfram's writeup of how earlier versions of GPT worked, for all its complexity it ultimately generated a probability distribution over 50,000 possible next tokens, given an input context. As impressive as its output may be to some, it is still stuck in the same kind of small box as a tictactoe program, except with 50,000 moves instead of 9 (maybe backgammon would be a better analogy since it has an element of randomness). Human linguistic behavior--which some linguists argue is primarily non-textual!--does not work this way; nor does human reasoning. The difference matters and couldn't be more important. LLMs play word games, but human life, which includes human reasoning, is not a game.

Fascinating reply, thanks for writing all that. The "small world"/"large world" distinction, funnily enough, describes how I've thought about the capabilities of LLMs (and other software) for a while, which is to say that programs without the capacity for self-modification (the way a biological brain has) fundamentally occupy a limited gamut of space. Engaging with them at first makes it *seem* like that space is endless, but the more you engage with them, the more you begin to start seeing the boundaries of what they're capable of, and recognizing the patterns of what they can produce.

Any given human might have a fairly limited thought space as well, but they still have the *ability* to push beyond their current space boundaries, and can be encouraged to do so (assuming they're willing to try, and not, like, spitefully opposed to the idea). Non-self-modifying software does not.

One implication here is that a piece of software written to function like a human brain (that is, able to have thousands of connections between "neuron"-equivalents, and having a node scale similar to a human brain, e.g. 100 billion+ nodes each with 2000+ connections) could end up thinking like a human, but so far no one's built anything remotely close to that, and you also have to account for all the different *kinds* of neurons as well as the mechanisms of sensory input and expressive output.

But it goes without saying that LLMs are not *remotely* like that.

The comparison to calculators is a false one. Arguably, calculators lead to a shift in skill or a shift in focus, but regardless they do not warp the entirety of human cognition. They don't degrade meta-cognitive skills such as critical thinking. Even so, if you lost some of your ability to add up numbers on paper or in your head because you use calculators a lot, you'd still function fine in the world. If, instead, you lost some of your critical thinking skills because you've gotten used to leaning on generative AI? Your life could become demonstrably harder and worse. This is an aside, but in The Next Apocalypse, Chris Bagley suggests that one feature that differentiates peoples who survived past societal collapse from those who did not was critical thinking skill. Anyway, the scope of calculators vs. generative AI is very different, so the potential consequences of becoming dependent on one versus the other technology are not comparable.

I think you are not conveying correctly just *how limited* this study is.

"critical thinking" is only measured in the context of the task that is offloaded to AI. So when people think more or less, it's just the (self-reported) amount on that specific task, which could mean e.g. verifying the information, proof-reading text, or (re-)writing the prompt.

People that are confident in the AI—that is, people that think the AI will do a good job at the task—then proceed to not check the output.

That's it.

You can just swap out "AI" with literally anything else and see that it's a banality: People that are confident in information on Wikipedia don't scroll down to read the references. People that are confident in their calculator don't verify the result with their abacus. People that are confident in their own spouse do not independently verify whether the front door has been locked. People that are confident that AI will do a task well do not also do the task themselves. No shit!

The other variable that was strongly (negatively) correlated was "confidence in self". Also super banal: People that think they can't do it themselves accept whatever the AI gives them.

Sure, for academics it's important bla, bla, bla, but for the public at large this study is a non-finding.
